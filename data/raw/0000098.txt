<p>Fine and good, all that, but not even half-way up that mountain of 100-pages is this babel:</p>
<blockquote><p>âPenitenziagite! Watch out for the draco who cometh in futurum to gnaw your anima! Death is super nos! Pray the Santo Pater come to liberar nos a malo and all our sin! <p>This is Eco at his most Joycean â make that most <em>Finnegans Wake</em> â and unless you have a degree in Romance Languages, good luck making sense of any of that.</p>
<p>As for âWhoâ would read this book: My father, who is the smartest man Iâve met, was undeterred by this book. <p>He regularly worked double-digit hours in his bakery six days a week, but when it was time to read <em>The Name of the Rose</em>, he sat propped up in bed, reading glasses on the tip of his nose and at least six volumes of the Encyclopedia around him and on top of my sleeping mother as a type of blanket. <p>And at the bakery my uncles, who shared my fatherâs penchant for late night reading, also discussed the difficulty of the novel â and all were relieved when the movie came out: âI can finally see the place heâs describing!,&#8221; my uncle Skip announced.</p>
<p>If the words themselves werenât enough to deter an audience, the references should have turned away an array of readers: hereâs three graphs from page 53:</p>
<blockquote><p>Celestine V was succeeded by Boniface VIII, and this Pope promptly demonstrated scant indulgence for Spirituals and Fraticelli in general: in the last years of the dying century he signed a bull, Firma cautela, in which with one stroke he condemned bizochi, vagabond mendicants who roamed about at the far edge of the Franciscan order, and the Spirituals themselves, who had left the life of the order and retired to a hermitage.</p>
<p>After the death of Boniface VIII, the Spirituals tried to obtain from certain of his successors, among them Clement V, permission to leave the order peaceably. <p>All cannot have proceeded smoothly, and many in the curia resisted. <p>Unless you are a medievalist with a working knowledge of the politics of the papacy, the above is almost as impenetrable as the Abbeyâs labyrinthine library.</p>
<p>And still people bought â and read â and struggled with this book. Eco himself has said that the book is âsimplyâ a âWhodunit?â and that is, again, a big part of its appeal: who is killing these poor monks?</p>
<p>A more sane reader might answer, âWho cares? I canât read Latin [which, get ready for this, is the language of the final lines of the book], and donât know anything about 14th-century Italian Church politics.â</p>
<p>Still, millions and millions read on.</p>
<p>And ultimately the book does not disappoint. Indeed, it dazzles. If ever there was a tour de force performance by a first-time novelist who created a literary and popular hitâmake that a âcraze,&#8221; given that the book spawned not only a movie, but a board- and video-gameâthis was it.</p>
<p>And that was it: all of Ecoâs novels that followed â with the very wide-berth possible exception of <em>Foucaultâs Pendulum</em>Â â have been critically, if only politely well-received but widely unread (do you know a soul who has bothered to read <em>Baudolino</em> or the ponderous <em>The Mysterious Flame of Queen Loana</em>? <p>Which leads to an obvious question: why didnât (and why doesnât) Eco simply write the sequel to <em>The Name of The Rose</em>? <p>No matter. We may never get the sequel. But re-reading <em>The Name of The Rose</em> is one of the pleasures that never disappoints, even a generation after it first appeared.</p>
<p>Publishing legend Robert Giroux once said, âA âclassicâ book is a book that stays in print.&#8221; On that level alone Ecoâs <em>The Name of The Rose</em> is classic â and one of the most enjoyable novels written in the past 30 years, in any language.</p>
