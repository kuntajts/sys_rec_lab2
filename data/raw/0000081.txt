                        <h5 class="ui author dividing header">
              By <a href="/authors/rw-flint" itemprop="author" rel="author">R.W. Flint</a>                              <span class="attribution">

                          <div class="introduction">
                <p>In 1947, Vladimir Nabokov wrote to former New Republic editor Edmund Wilson to tell him he was working on "a short novel about a man who liked little girlsâand it's going to be called The Kingdom by the Sea." By 1955 that novel appeared in printâbut with a different title: Lolita. As soon as the book's subject matter became widely known it garnered hatred and bowdlerization by those who looked right past Nabokov's elegant language and saw, as one editor put it, "filth." In 1957, our reviewer was forced to use a censored version of an excerpt of the book for his review, noting that this edition was "purged of its fleshier parts," and took a different tack. "To dwell on the bookâs more lurid side," he said "is to connive with witlessness" and declared the novelÂ "genius."</p><p>To mark its 100th anniversary, The New Republic isÂ <a href="http://www.newrepublic.com/tags/100-years-100-stories">republishing a collection of its most memorable articles</a>. This week's theme: Banned Books.</p><p>This piece originally appeared in The New Republic on June 17, 1957.</p>              </div>
                                    <div class="body" itemprop="articleBody" >
              <div class="pull-right-wide"><img width="361" height="102" src="/sites/default/files/u36457/screen_shot_2014-09-22_at_3.36.14_pm.png" title="Lolita-expurgated" alt="Lolita, expurgated"></div><p><span class="dropcap">T</span>he second <em>Anchor Review</em>, a pocket-sized magazine-and-reprint-journal of high standards, mildly radical leanings, and rare appearance, would be something to buy even without its 90-page excerpt from the notorious <em>Lolita</em>. But emasculated as it is, this first American edition of Vladimir Nabokovâs novel is a major literary event, worth all the attention we can spare. Circulated over here in its Paris edition, it moved one critic to say that âit flames with a tremendous perversity of an unexpected kindâ¦â and is ââ¦ just about the funniest book I remember having readâ; another critic to describe its action as âremorseless, simple and inevitable, like a Greek tragedy; or else a mass of absurdities, coincidences, and perverse exfoliations, like a Greek tragedyâ; a third critic to decide that â<em>Lolita </em>is&nbsp;partly a masterpiece of grotesque comedy, partly an unsubdued wilderness where the wolf howls<span class="em">&mdash;</span>a real wolf howling for a real Red Riding Hood.â</p><p>When Nabokovâs first English novel, <em>The Real Life of Sebastian Knight</em>, appeared during the forties, Edmund Wilson wrote:</p><blockquote><p>If I say that Nabokov, who was educated in Englandâ¦ turns out to be a master of English prose<span class="em">&mdash;</span>the most extraordinary phenomenon of the kind since Conrad<span class="em">&mdash;</span>this is likely to sound incredible. If I say that Nabokov is something like Proust, something like Franz Kafka, and probably, something like Gogol, I shall suggest an imitative patchwork, where Nabokov is as completely himself as any of these others<span class="em">&mdash;</span>a man with a unique sensibility and a unique story to tell.</p></blockquote><p>To complete my chorus of witnesses, here is Mr. Nabokov himself (from an amusing essay on <em>Lolita</em> included by Anchor):</p><blockquote><p>After Olympia Press, in Paris, published the book, an American critic suggested that <em>Lolita</em> was the record of my love affair with the romantic novel. The substitution âEnglish languageâ for âromantic novelâ would make this elegant formula more correct. But here I feel my voice rising to too strident a pitch. None of my American friends have read my Russian books and thus every appraisal on the strength of my English ones is bound to be out of focus. My private tragedy, which cannot, and indeed should not, be anybodyâs concern, is that I had to abandon my natural idiom, my untrammeled, rich, and infinitely docile Russian tongue for a second-rate brand of English, devoid of any of those apparatuses<span class="em">&mdash;</span>the baffling mirror, the black velvet backdrop, the implied associations and traditions<span class="em">&mdash;</span>which the native illusionist, frac-tails flying, can magically use to transcend the heritage in his own way.</p></blockquote><p>This unwarranted fit of modesty on Mr. Nabokovâs part at least gives us a special clue to the genius of his book. Since he could not develop <em>a</em> style of this imputed weight, he did the next best thing and forged a prose of spectacular vitality and full of surprises. His <em>Nicolai Gogol</em> (New Directions, 1944) is the best treatise on comedy I know of, and a kind of book, one must remember when comparing two men, that Gogol could not conceivably have written himself. In a test with Gogol, Mr. Nabokov makes up in learned wit what he lacks in poetry or control. He is nothing if not intelligent; the parallel with Gogol has to be found behind yet another set of ironic baffles, in a really fiendish, lyric delight in the bottom absurdity of things. But the parallel need not be drawn too far; Nabokov warns against it. He presents Gogol, in fact, as a monster of self-delusion as well as one of the supreme masters of Russian fiction. But he also shows, in this model of critical biography, a standard of comedy (ââ¦ one likes to recall that the difference between the comic side of things, and their cosmic side, depends upon one sibilantâ¦â) that springs to wonderful life in <em>Lolita</em>.</p><p>What makes the book âflame,â I think, is first of all a love affair with the real America. (âNothing is more exhilarating than philistine vulgarityâ¦. I chose American motels instead of Swiss hotels or English inns only because I am trying to be an American writer and claim only the same rights that other American writers enjoy.â) It is an America where language and event make a seamless web of wonders, terrors, revelations, and portents. English, for Mr. Nabokov, is an instrument for the wildest and most mysteriously fitting shifts of tone, the most cheerfully extroverted, slang-relishing, literate verbal tomfoolery.</p><p>Since the Anchor <em>Lolita</em> is purged of its fleshier parts, since the author defends himself very ably, and since to dwell on the bookâs more lurid side is to connive with witlessness, I shall skip all this. Suffice it here to say that Lolitaâs chief actor, Humbert Humbert, a Swiss âsalad of racial genes,â is afflicted with about equal degrees of wit, ennui, taste, and a hideously overt form of nympholepsy; the disease of Ruskin and Lewis Carroll given free and tender rein in a wilderness of American motels, suburbs, and progressive institutions. <em>Lolita</em> is a burlesque of Freudianism that Freud would have had to enjoy. I can respect Fred Dupeeâs view, in his Anchor Preface to the book, that âthere is frequently a disquieting note of unresolved tensionâ; but am inclined to think that the burlesque takes one more turn, at least, than we would expect of any previous example of the âconfessional novelâ or â<em>roman noirâ</em> (Mr. Dupeeâs terms). All Mr. Nabokov has to confess, I think, is imagination enough to project the charms of a Phedre or Cleopatra into the skin of an odious suburban bobby-soxer. How perverse this is each reader must decide for himself; but few milder absurdities would have suited Mr. Nabokovâs mad purposes so well.</p>
